Combat Stages:

Combat is divided into a series of three-second turns. Each combat turn has three stages to make it easier to keep track of things.



Initiative

This stage organizes the turn and is when you declare your character’s action.

You must declare what your character does, you must also state if any multiple actions will be performed like dodging and attacking at the same time, if Disciplines will be activated, if Willpower points will be spent, or if the character wants to delay the turn.

After declaring the action, you wait until the attack stage to implement that action.

Everyone, player and Storyteller character alike, rolls one die and adds it to their initiative rating (Dexterity + Wits);

Characters declare in reverse order of initiative, thus giving faster characters the opportunity to react to slower characters’ actions.

Finally, all additional actions that turn (including actions gained through Celerity) occur at the end of the turn. If two or more characters take multiple actions, the actions occur in order of initiative rating. An exception is made for defensive multiple actions, such as multiple dodges, which happen when they need to happen in order to avert attack.

Remember that wound penalties subtract directly from a character’s initiative rating, while Celerity dots that aren’t being used for extra actions add to it

The attack stage:

Attacks are the meat of the combat turn. You use a certain Attribute/Ability combination depending on the type of combat in which your character is engaged.

If your character doesn’t have points in the necessary Ability, fall back to the Attribute (Dexterity).

Close Combat: Dexterity + Brawl (unarmed) or Dexterity + Melee (armed).

Ranged Combat: Dexterity + Firearms (guns) or Dexterity + Athletics (thrown weapons).

Most attacks are made versus difficulty 6.

This can be adjusted however for situational modifiers like long range or cramped quarters.

In ranged combat, your weapon may modify your dice pool or difficulty (due to rate of fire, a targeting scope, etc.); check the weapon’s statistics for details.

Remember you can abort your action at any time to do a defensive maneuver as long as your character hasn't made an action yet.

Aborting requires you to roll a willpower rating of 6 or use a willpower point. Doing so allows you to do a block, dodge or parry. (See Defensive Maneuvers)


Resolution:

During this stage, you determine the damage inflicted by your character’s attack, and the Storyteller describes what occurs in the turn.

Normally, additional successes gained on a Trait roll simply mean that you do exceptionally well. In combat, each extra success you get on an attack roll equals an additional die you add automatically to your damage dice pool. This creates cinematic and often fatal combat.

After damage have been rolled against your character, you are allowed to do a soak roll.


Soaking:

Characters can resist a certain degree of physical punishment; this is called soaking damage.

Your character’s soak dice pool is equal to her Stamina. A normal human can soak only bashing damage. A vampire (or other supernatural being) is tougher, and can thus use soak dice against lethal damage.

After an attack hits and inflicts damage, the defender may make a soak roll to resist. This is considered a reflexive action; characters need not take an action or split a dice pool to soak. Each soak success subtracts one die from the total damage inflicted.

Unless otherwise stated, soak rolls are made versus difficulty 6. As with damage rolls, soak rolls may not botch, only fail.

Aggravated damage may be soaked only with the Discipline of Fortitude.

The Discipline Fortitude also adds to the defender’s soak rating against bashing or lethal damage (so a character with Stamina 3 and Fortitude 2 has five soak dice against bashing and lethal damage, but only two soak dice against aggravated damage).


Quick summary:

Stage 1: Initiative

Roll initiative (1x D10 + Dexterity + Wits).

Everyone declares their actions in reverse order to let faster character act on slower characters. The character with highest initiative performs her action first. Actions can be delayed to any time later in the order of initiative.

Declare any multiple actions, reducing dice pools accordingly. Declare Discipline activation and Willpower expenditure.

Stage 2: Attack

Uarmed close-combat attacks, roll Dexterity + Brawl.

Armed close-combat attacks, roll Dexterity + Melee.

Guns, roll Dexterity + Firearms.

Thrown weapons, roll Dexterity + Athletics.

A character can abort to a defensive action (block, dodge, parry) at any time before her action is performed, as long as you make a successful Willpower roll (or a Willpower point is spent).

Stage 3: Resolution

Determine total damage effect (weapon type or maneuver), adding any extra dice gained from successes on the attack roll.

Targets may attempt to soak damage, if possible.


Example play:

Stage 1: Initiative

Alice got 8.

Bob got 7.

Charlie got 6.

Danielle got 5.

Danielle: "I attack Alice."

Charlie: "I attack Dan."

Bob: "I activate Celerity. Using Celerity I spend a blood point for one extra action. With my normal action, I attack Charlie."

Alice: "I attack Dan."

Bob: "With my Celerity Action I attack Danielle."

Alice: "I make my action a multiple action - and I additionally parry against Danielle"

Stage 2: Attack

Alice is doing multiple actions and reduces her die pool down to the action that has the lowest die pool (the parry). She allocates the die in that lowest die pool between her attack and the defense.

Alice attacks dan with the dice she has allocated, Dan may abort to defense, but chooses not to. The attack roll is resolved, and Alice gets one success.

Bob is going to attack Charlie. Charlie aborts his action to do a defensive maneuver - A dodge. Charlie rolls his willpower (difficulty 6) to actually do the abort and succeeds.

Bob rolls his attack roll. Charlie rolls his Dodge. Charlie got more successes than Bob and thus Bob misses Charlie.

Danielle attacks Alice. Alice tries to parry as part of her multiple action. Danielle rolls attack, Alice rolls her parry with her allocated dice. Danielle has more successes, partly because Alice had too few dice in her dice pool to match her. Alice fails her defensive maneuver.

Bob rolls his Celerity action attack on Danielle.

Stage 3: Resolution

Alice rolls her damage, then Dan rolls to soak.

Bob cannot roll damage on Charlie as Charlie dodged it.

Danielle rolls her damage because Alice failed the parry, Alice rolls to soak.

Bob rolls his damage on Danielle. Danielle rolls to soak.
